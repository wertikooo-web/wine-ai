Moldova has four wine-growing regions: Central, Southern, South-Eastern and Northern (also described elsewhere as Bălţi/north, Codru/central, Ştefan Vodă/south-east and Cahul/south, with Cahul considered especially important for red sweet and semi-sweet wines). Since 2013 Moldova has applied an EU-style system of Protected Geographical Indications (PGI) covering three wine-growing areas — Valul lui Traian, Ştefan Vodă and Codru — plus a separate geographical name, Divin, for the country's aged wine-spirit (brandy). These four geographical indications received legal protection within Moldova in 2013 and were formally recognized within the European Union as of 18 October 2016. The first PGI-quality wines (1.6 million bottles) were produced from the 2015 harvest, once the "Organization of the winegrowing market" technical regulation was approved in July 2015. In 2018, PGI-designated vineyard area grew 22% to 9,200 hectares and the number of PGI winemakers rose 15% to 71, most registered in the Valul lui Traian region (35%).

**PGI Valul lui Traian** lies in the Southern wine region and covers the wine centres of Leova, Cantemir, Cahul, Comrat, Ceadîr-Lunga, Taraclia and Vulcăneşti — about 43,200 hectares of designated area, named after the ancient Trajan's Wall fortification that runs through it. Red wines make up 60% of production here, white 35% and rosé 5%; local varieties like Fetească Albă and Fetească Regală are commonly blended in. Leading varieties by area include Cabernet Sauvignon (896 ha), Merlot (693 ha), Sauvignon Blanc (371 ha), Chardonnay (300 ha), Muscat Ottonel (210 ha), Saperavi (132 ha), Aligoté (100 ha), Fetească Neagră (56 ha), Riesling (55 ha) and Pinot Gris (53 ha). This region is also considered the cradle of Moldova's fortified wines (Ciumai, Trifeşti, Borceag, Slobozia Mare).

**PGI Ştefan Vodă** is in the South-Eastern region (Ştefan Vodă, Căuşeni, Basarabeasca and Cimişlia wine centres, about 10,000 ha of designated area) and is regarded as the birthplace of the Rară Neagră grape variety. Its climate is compared to Bordeaux, France, moderated by the Nistru (Dniester) River and Black Sea breezes. White grapes make up 42% and red 58% here; Cabernet Sauvignon leads, followed by Chardonnay (now ahead of Merlot), then Sauvignon Blanc, Pinot Gris, Muscat Ottonel, Malbec, Saperavi, Traminer and Rară Neagră.

**PGI Codru** spans the Central, South-Eastern and Northern regions (Hânceşti, Răzeni, Ialoveni, Bulboaca, Chişinău, Romăneşti, Nisporeni, Călăraşi, Orhei, Teleneşti, Dubăsari, Tiraspol, Făleşti, Bălţi and Rîşcani wine centres — about 61,200 ha designated area). It is home to the largest underground wine-storage galleries in the country: Cricova, Mileştii Mici and Brăneşti, together holding more than 2 million collection bottles. The Russian imperial Romanov family founded their own winery here, in the village of Romăneşti. The climate is mild (10-12°C average annual temperature, 2,100-2,200 sunshine hours/year, 550-700mm annual rainfall), and white varieties (63%) exceed red (37%) here, unusually with Cabernet Sauvignon still the single largest variety by area (335 ha), followed by Chardonnay (334 ha), Riesling (260 ha), Sauvignon Blanc (218 ha), Merlot (209 ha), Aligoté (159 ha), Muscat Ottonel (147 ha), Pinot Noir (124 ha), Rkatsiteli (123 ha) and Fetească Albă (40 ha).

**PGI Divin** covers the entire territory of Moldova and applies specifically to the country's aged wine-spirit/brandy, called "Divin" (the name was chosen to avoid confusion with French "cognac" while asserting a distinct national identity). Divin is produced by fractional distillation of dry wine made from authorized grape varieties (Aligoté, Chardonnay, Fetească Albă, Rkatsiteli, Sauvignon Blanc, Ugni Blanc and others) and aged at least 3 years in oak. Quality categories by minimum aging period are: V.S. (Very Special, 3 years), V.S.O.P. (Very Superior Old Pale, 5 years), X.O. (Extra Old, 7 years) and X.X.O. (Extra Extra Old, 20 years). The first wine-spirit factories in Moldova date to 1896-1929; some Divin batches produced in the post-war period have matured for over 40-50 years.
