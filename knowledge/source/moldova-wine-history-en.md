Grape seeds found on clay pot sherds on the territory of Moldova have been dated to roughly 3750-3500 BC, evidence that winemaking on these lands began 4-5 thousand years ago when the Dacians discovered wine production from grapes. Winemaking intensified when Greek colonists arrived on the Black Sea coast toward the end of the 3rd century BC and shared their winemaking traditions with the local population. Around 50 BC, king Burebista, worried about excessive wine consumption, ordered the Dacians and Getae to cut down their vineyards and stop drinking wine; this restriction lasted about 150 years until the Roman conquest of Dacia by Emperor Trajan in 106 AD. The Romans then actively stimulated viticulture, introducing new cultivation systems and grape varieties from Rome that survived into the twentieth century, and much of today's Moldovan winemaking terminology has Latin/Roman roots.

With the founding of the Principality of Moldova in the 14th century, viticulture grew rapidly, reaching a peak in the 15th century during the reign of Stephen the Great (Ştefan cel Mare), who imported new vine varieties and encouraged quality wine production. A "cult of wine" formed among the Boyars, extensive underground galleries were built for storage, and Moldova began exporting wine to Poland and the Principality of Moscow from the 14th century onward, marking the start of its history as a wine exporter. At the beginning of the 16th century Moldova became a vassal of the Ottoman Empire, which restricted winemaking, though the Orthodox Church helped preserve the tradition since wine was used in liturgy; strict quality standards for church wine were set out in the "Slujebnicul" in 1699.

The annexation of Bessarabia (Basarabia) to the Russian Empire in 1812 opened a new expansion period: French varieties were imported, and vineyard area grew from about 14,000 hectares (1 million buckets of wine) in 1837 to roughly 74,000 desetine and 15 million buckets by the start of the 20th century, with two-thirds exported, including to France during its own phylloxera-driven shortage. Purcari red wines won a gold medal at the 1878 Paris World Exposition and were purchased for the Russian imperial court; the Romanov family founded their own winery, Romăneşti. Phylloxera devastated Moldovan vineyards toward the end of the 19th century; replanting with grafted rootstock restored them by 1906, and by 1914 most vineyards in the Russian Empire were located in Bessarabia. World Wars I and II caused major destruction and stagnation.

After WWII, a large replanting campaign in the 1950s brought total vineyard area to 220,000 hectares by 1960, making Moldova the largest wine producer among Soviet republics — every second bottle of wine and every third bottle of sparkling wine in the USSR was produced there. The underground galleries of Cricova, Mileştii Mici and Brăneşti were expanded during this period into storage cities stretching hundreds of kilometers, eventually housing around 30,000 tonnes of varietal wines and about 2 million collection bottles. In 1985 Mikhail Gorbachev's Soviet anti-alcoholism campaign led to the destruction of large numbers of vineyards across Moldova (roughly 30% of vineyards were destroyed between 1985 and 1987).

After independence in 1991, wineries were privatized by the mid-1990s and new owners invested in modern equipment; significant vineyard replanting with European clonal varieties took place 2000-2005. Russia imposed wine import embargoes in 2006 (lasting about two years) and again in September 2013, both times causing sharp export contractions (wine production's share of GDP fell from about 9% in 2005 to 2.3% in 2007 after the first embargo) but ultimately pushing the industry to diversify into EU and other markets, invest in quality, and move away from its Soviet-era reputation as a bulk raw-material supplier. In 2013 the National Office of Vine and Wine (NOVW/ONVV) was created as a public-private structure to manage the "Wine of Moldova" country brand and support export diversification; by 2018 wine exports of $137.9 million reached over 50 countries, with the EU (39.6% of export value) surpassing the CIS (36.7%) for the first time.
